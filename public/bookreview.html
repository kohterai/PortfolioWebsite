The Republic

Plato (Translated by Tom Griffith)

December 6, 2018

This is the first philosophical text I’ve read. It was difficult at first to get used to the style of writing, especially since Plato makes lots of references to Greek mythology, as well as other philosophers of the time. The text is written in Socratic dialogue, where one of the characters (Socrates), asks questions to keep the conversation going forward.

I picked up this book in hopes to get a better understanding of moral philosophy, hoping to learn what is “good” and what is “bad”. Instead what I got was a discourse that went a layer deeper, discussing whether it’s important to be just or if the illusion of being just is enough. Sometimes the dialog makes sudden pivots and the main point is discussed in convoluted ways that it makes me have to reread the text multiple times to understand what is being said.

Then Plato goes on to discuss what a perfect city would look like, and different forms of governments that can exist. Great time is spent talking about what the ruler should be like, and what their education should be. I think Plato clearly takes an elitist, or classist stance on this. He does make a point that a ruler should rule for the best interest of the people, and not for his or her own benefits. This means the ruler should not be entitled to wealth.

His way of imagining an ideal city ground up made me think about certain norms and beliefs that are set in brick today. For example, Plato goes a great deal to moderate the types of Heroes that are idolized in his city. What kinds of people are considered Heroes today? Of course, this depends by culture but it’s possible that these are thoughts and expectations that are embedded in us by certain rulers, intentionally or unintentionally.

I find it fun to think how humans over 2000 years ago were able to think so sophisticatedly. It reminds me of Homo Deus and how Harari starts his book off explaining to us how humans have biologically not “upgraded” since Homo Sapiens emerged (us). To read such an ancient text with so much thought is evidence for that.

Plato did feel a bit narcissistic at parts, especially when preaching the importance of philosophers in society. Also, parts of the Socratic dialogue where Socrates would say something in a convoluted way and the listener would respond “I don’t understand, can you simplify”, and Socrates would say something along the lines of “It’s already so simplified, I can’t believe you don’t understand”. This sort of back and forth felt like a simple ego boost for himself.

Overall, I enjoyed reading the book and gave me a lot of new thoughts about how our society is constructed, and how so many forms of governance can exist. Plato also does talk about gender equality and communal parenting, which feels like rather “innovative” social thinking, at least in today’s society. Griffith made the translation with an emphasis to make the text feel like a conversation as much as possible. I found the translation to be pretty straightforward and easy to read. With that said, if I were to read another philosophical text, I would love to try a more recent philosopher.

Bullshit Jobs

David Graeber

November 11, 2018

Bullshit Jobs is a recent book (2018) written by a professor at the London School of Economics. Graeber describes a phenomenon that he describes called “bullshit jobs” which he feels that no one is talking about but is a modern day epidemic.

First, he begins by describing what he considers at a bullshit job and describes the kinds of bullshit jobs that exist in the market. Then he discusses how the people doing these bullshit jobs feel about them, and why they continue doing them. Since Graeber has a background in Anthropology, he brings out the anthropological side out to theorize why bullshit jobs has proliferated in developed countries, and finally what we can do about that.

I picked this book up since it was available at a bookstore in London and since I was in the midst of thinking about what it means to work and what jobs are, that it would be an interesting read. Graeber bases most of his theories off of comments and stories he received from various people who feel that they have bullshit jobs.

First and foremost, Graeber says that in order for a job to be a bullshit job, the employee themselves must think that it’s a bullshit job. If they feel that it’s a bullshit job, at that point it is humiliating to keep working in such a job, but the argument goes that the way our capitalistic society is structured, prevents these people from picking up another job while maintaining a decent standard of living. This is the premise of the book.

I must say that this is an incredibly sensationalist piece of writing, but I think he does present points that many people can empathize with, and even if the problem is not as wide spread as the author believes, I do believe that these types of “bullshit jobs” do exist out there, and that it is not productive.

If a person believes that their job is bullshit, I do feel that it is their responsibility to do something about it. But with that said, if the whole system is ambivalent to this type of problem to the extend the author describes, then I do feel bad for those stuck in these jobs. I’m also curious why the author is so emotionally invested in this matter. Does he feel like he has a bullshit job himself? Or does he feel that he is a victim of people working on these bullshit jobs? It’s unclear.

Personally, this book was food for thought to think about why people work. People work for many different reasons. Work could be for money. But work could also be to gain societal respect and status. Or work could be a place for one’s sense of belonging. But I do agree that work for the sake of work can be problematic. Perhaps another angle to the problem he describes is to think about why people feel that their work is bullshit. Maybe there has always been bullshit jobs. Maybe people just feel that they are meant to be doing “more” or have a greater sense of purpose. Maybe it’s social media that is making people feel that they are not living the lives they want to live. Maybe it’s not work itself that is the problem but the people. I say this because many forms of work can be classified as “bullshit” if you ask enough “why” questions.

Graeber is brave to write a book about this. At the end he does bring up the idea of universal basic income. Although he does state that he doesn’t want this book to be categorized as a “universal basic income” book, the phenomena and the ideas he discusses are clearly relevant if someday we do shift to a lifestyle of UBI. Because the culture of work is so deeply engrained in society and culture, that if we were to admit that most work is unnecessary, or just a product of bureaucracy, or isn’t really productive for society, then we really do need to rethink our values and our purpose of living. Graeber does make us think about the value of work through his book. I do think the book is too extreme and makes a lot of strong statements that are quite questionable though.

Simon Garfield

October 10, 2018

When I was at the bookstore, I decided that I would try picking up a book from a genre I usually don’t read (this eliminates tech, popular science, self-help, and design books). In fact, I’ve had thoughts in the past that I would have loved to pursue the profession of a cartographer if I was born several centuries ago. Anyway, I decided that this book would be a change from my usual reading list.

I think a more appropriate title for the book would be “Short Stories Involving Maps”. I thought it would be a more comprehensive historical account of mapmaking, but it’s actually little snippets and stories that involve various maps. For example, the story behind the sale of the Mappa Mundi and its significance, to seeing the world from the earliest accounts of the world of Eratosthenes and Ptolemy. Towards the end of the book, the stories become more meta-cartography like cartography in video games or mapping of the brain.

As a person with no prior knowledge of cartography, it was quite a fun read, although I did not enjoy the meta-cartography stories towards the end. The book started to feel quite long and I had to skim through the last chapters, since they didn’t feel as exciting (e.g. London Subway Map, Maps in Video Maps, GPS systems) With that said, I did find the stories from the earliest days of cartography quite exciting. For example, to imagine these cartographers sailing into literally unknown, sounds like quite an adventure. We take it for granted now that we know what the world looks like, but to imagine there was a time that this was not the case, is fascinating. Perhaps it’s similar to the way we think of the universe right now.

Some of the stories involve cartographers creating fictitious landmarks on maps, that end up being duplicated for centuries. For example, a fictitious mountain called the Mountains of Kong that were created in Africa or California being represented as an island. We take accurate maps for granted, and don’t even consider it a possibility that what we see on Google Maps may be inaccurate (or at least I don’t). So, in many ways, having charted most of the world takes away the romance of having so many “unknowns” about our world. This is true in many levels (not just in physical maps of the world).

One of the chapters was about John Snow and the Cholera map. Of course, I’m familiar with this from Tufte’s books but it did feel good to have some overlap there. My biggest criticism, which I started feeling after being halfway done with the book is that a lot of the stories are heavily centered around cartography stories in England. Of course, there are a few stories that involve the US but it would be difficult to say that it represents a global history of mapmaking. Also, I wish the book came with more pictures of the maps that it talks about (and in greater detail). It’s tricky to imagine the maps without actually seeing them.

The chapters are short and self-contained, so I do think it’s a good book for bedtime reading.

Matthew Walker

September 3, 2018

I've developed many personal theories about sleep during my High School years. I've had times when I've been frustrated by the fact that I need to sleep everyday. During my college years I've realized that the amount of sleep I get affects my mood and performance the next day. This book had demystified everything I've ever wondered about the purpose and meaning of sleep.

My biggest takeaway is that having quality sleep everyday is incredibly important for humans. It is so important to us for our physical and mental health. The book makes many references to experiments that have been conducted and other humans and animals that intentionally disrupt sleep (in many different ways). Walker also focuses a lot of the discourse on how we as a society have been neglecting sleep so much. We fail to acknowledge the importance of it, and also fail to educate ourselves about it despite the fact that we spend a good 1/3 of our lives sleeping.

He has a lot of practical ideas that can help society value sleep more, and realistic ways of how technology can better people's quality of sleep.

There seems to be a lot more interest in people tracking their sleep patterns on their phones lately. It seems like we are becoming more aware of how important sleep is to us, and making efforts to treating our sleep with respect.

My only criticism for this book is that a lot of it is much more technical and scientific than I prefer (lots of words for parts of the brain, lots of words for different types of chemicals -- it makes me sleep reading) as well as Walker coming off as peachy at times, making sleep sound like the most important thing to humans (I am convinced it is important but there are other important things to our well being as well).
